Navigation Safety Is a Control Chain

From the voyage instruction to the post-arrival review, every passage must be appraised, planned, verified, briefed, monitored and reviewed. The true danger is not the sea itself, but a change that is unseen, unrecorded or unchallenged.

Safety is not luck. It is control that can be proven.

The purpose of navigation safety procedure is to prevent collision, grounding, contact, pollution and navigation-related operational loss. Every decision gate must leave evidence so the route, tide window, alarms, handover, pilotage difference, deviation and review can be reconstructed.

Master Authority Delay, stop, anchor, deviate or request tugs when safety requires it.
Technology Role ECDIS supports judgement. It never replaces lookout or professional decision-making.
Evidence Loop Every rise in risk needs a record and a learning action.

The Six-Part Navigation Safety Control Chain

Treat the passage as a route that must keep being challenged, not as a chart that stops changing after one approval.

01

Appraise: See the limits first

Vessel condition, draft, stability, charts, publications, tides, warnings, port limits, security and environmental requirements must be confirmed before the route is drawn.

  • Confirm official charts, nautical publications and navigational warnings.
  • Identify IALA, radar marks, fixing methods and restricted waters.
  • Include safety, security, environmental and contact-point data in the voyage file.

Danger usually appears first as a small deviation.

Equipment, tide windows, traffic, visibility, pilot advice and fatigue can each turn an approved route into a deteriorating situation.

Collision Risk

Maintain proper lookout, radar plotting, CPA/TCPA assessment and COLREG judgement. AIS is supporting information only; it cannot replace visual and radar confirmation.

Immediate action: safe speed, early avoiding action and call the Master when required.

Decision Gate: Can we enter restricted waters?

If UKC, tide window, abort point, engine readiness, manual steering, turning room or Master presence on the bridge is unclear, the procedure requires the vessel to hold outside the safety boundary.

Arrival is not the end. It is the safety starting point for the next voyage.

The Master should lead the voyage review with available navigating officers and engine-room input. Review near misses, alarm overload, pilotage deviations, missed appraisal factors, route reasonableness and new port information. A strong safety culture keeps the lesson in the record, not as a hidden burden for the next watch.
